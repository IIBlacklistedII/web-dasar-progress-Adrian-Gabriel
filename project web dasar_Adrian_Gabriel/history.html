Syria Through the Ages

3000 BCE

The Ancient Kingdom Era

Ebla and Mari flourish as global trading empires. The world's earliest written diplomatic tablets and alphabet samples emerge from these Syrian roots.

1200 BCE

The Phoenician & Aramean Age

Following the Bronze Age collapse, Aramean kingdoms unify inland trade routes, standardizing the Aramaic language. Concurrently, coastal cities advance global maritime navigation and spread the earliest linear phonetic alphabet.

333 BCE

Hellenistic Conquest

Alexander the Great conquers the region, initiating centuries of Greek influence. The Seleucid Empire establishes Antioch as its capital, transforming Syria into a massive epicenter of Hellenistic art, science, and cultural synthesis.

64 BCE

Roman & Byzantine Rule

Syria becomes a crucial province of the Roman Empire. Magnificent cities like Palmyra grow into wealthy trading hubs matching Rome itself.

661 CE

The Umayyad Caliphate

Damascus becomes the capital of an empire stretching from Spain to India. The Grand Umayyad Mosque is constructed as an architectural marvel.

1187 CE

The Crusader & Ayyubid Wars

Syria turns into a central theater of conflict during the Crusades. Sultan Saladin unifies regional factions from his base in Damascus, constructing legendary structural reinforcements at the Aleppo Citadel and capturing major fortresses.

1516 CE

Ottoman Integration

Syria is incorporated into the Ottoman Empire. Damascus and Aleppo become the premier terminal hubs for trans-desert Silk Road caravans and official global pilgrimage assembly routes, sparking an boom in souk architecture.

1946 CE

Modern Independence

Syria officially secures full sovereignty, declaring the evacuation of foreign troops and establishing its modern republic identity.
